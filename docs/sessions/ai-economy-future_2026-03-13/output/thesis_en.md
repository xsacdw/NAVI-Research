## Abstract

Artificial intelligence (AI) technology heralds the most fundamental economic paradigm shift since the Industrial Revolution of the 18th century. This study systematically analyzes the multidimensional impact of AI on macroeconomics, labor markets, industrial structure, and income distribution. Goldman Sachs projects that generative AI could increase global GDP by 7% (approximately $7 trillion), while MIT's Acemoglu conservatively estimates the GDP growth effect at approximately 1% over the next decade. In the labor market, the World Economic Forum predicts the creation of 170 million new jobs and the displacement of 92 million jobs between 2025 and 2030, while the IMF warns that 40% of global employment is exposed to AI. This study comparatively analyzes these divergent projections, draws implications for the Korean economy in particular, and presents economic policy challenges for the AI era. Analysis results suggest that AI's economic impact is more likely to be determined by the speed and direction of institutional responses than by the technology itself, with education system redesign, social safety net reinforcement, and expansion of international cooperation emerging as key challenges.

**Keywords:** Artificial Intelligence, Economic Future, Labor Market, GDP, Automation, Income Inequality, Generative AI, Economic Policy

## Chapter 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Research Background

The economic history of human civilization is a succession of paradigm shifts driven by technological innovation. The invention of the steam engine in the 18th century transformed agricultural economies into industrial ones, electricity and the internal combustion engine in the 20th century established mass production systems, and the digital revolution of the late 20th century ushered in the information economy era. Now, 21st-century artificial intelligence (AI) technology portends economic changes broader and more profound than any previous technological revolution.

In particular, the rapid advancement of generative AI, triggered by the release of ChatGPT in 2022, has shifted the discourse on AI's economic impact from theoretical possibility to practical urgency. McKinsey Global Institute (2023) estimates that generative AI can create $2.6 to $4.4 trillion in annual value across 63 use cases, a scale comparable to the UK's entire GDP as of 2021. IDC (2024) projects that AI-related business spending will have a cumulative global economic impact of $19.9 trillion by 2030.

However, alongside these optimistic projections, serious concerns are simultaneously being raised. MIT's Daron Acemoglu (2024) presents a conservative forecast that AI's GDP growth effect will amount to approximately 1% over the next decade, pointing out the possibility that technology's productivity enhancement effects may be overestimated. The IMF (2024) warns that approximately 40% of global employment is exposed to AI, potentially exacerbating income inequality. According to the Korea Development Institute (KDI), it is estimated that technically 70% or more of tasks can be automated in 38.8% of domestic jobs.

### 1.2 Research Objectives

The objectives of this study can be summarized in four points.

First, to comparatively analyze the projections of major institutions and scholars regarding the impact of AI technology advancement on macroeconomic indicators, particularly GDP growth. Second, to conduct a multilayered analysis of AI automation's impact on the labor market from the perspectives of job displacement, new job creation, and wage structure changes. Third, to identify the mechanisms of industrial structural transformation and income inequality caused by AI. Fourth, to draw implications for the Korean economy based on these analyses and present policy recommendations.

### 1.3 Research Scope and Methodology

This study adopts a Systematic Literature Review methodology, systematically reviewing major academic papers, international organization reports, and consulting firm studies published since 2020. The analysis covers reports from international organizations such as the IMF, World Bank, and OECD; reports from private research institutions such as Goldman Sachs, McKinsey, and PwC; academic research from MIT, Stanford, and others; and reports from domestic research institutions such as KDI and the Korea Labor Institute.

The research scope temporally focuses on medium-to-long-term projections from the present (2026) to 2040, and spatially targets the global economy while particularly emphasizing the derivation of implications for the Korean economy.

### 1.4 Paper Structure

This paper consists of ten chapters. Chapter 2 surveys the current state and outlook of AI technology development, Chapter 3 analyzes GDP growth projections, and Chapter 4 examines labor market impacts. Chapters 5 and 6 address industrial structural transformation and income inequality respectively, Chapter 7 discusses economic policy challenges, and Chapter 8 addresses the reorganization of the global economic order. Chapter 9 draws implications for the Korean economy, and Chapter 10 presents conclusions and recommendations.

## Chapter 2. Current State and Outlook of AI Technology Development

### 2.1 Trajectory of AI Technology Evolution

The development of artificial intelligence technology can be broadly divided into four stages. First-generation AI (1950s–1980s) consisted of rule-based expert systems—limited systems that operated according to pre-programmed logic. Second-generation AI (1990s–2010s) was machine learning-based, equipped with the ability to learn patterns from data but remaining confined to task-specific 'narrow AI.'

Third-generation AI (2017–present) is represented by the emergence of the Transformer architecture and the development of Large Language Models (LLMs). Starting with Google's "Attention Is All You Need" paper in 2017, increasingly powerful models have emerged, including GPT-3 (2020), GPT-4 (2023), Gemini (2023–2024), and Claude 3 (2024). These generative AI systems possess general-purpose capabilities to understand and generate diverse forms of content including text, images, code, and music.

Fourth-generation AI has not yet been realized, but development toward Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)—AI with human-level or superior general intelligence—is anticipated. Major AI laboratories such as OpenAI, Google DeepMind, and Anthropic have explicitly stated AGI development as their ultimate goal, and some experts project that AGI-level AI could emerge in the 2030s.

The economic implications of this technological evolution are profound. While previous generations of AI contributed primarily to automating repetitive, routine tasks, generative AI is expanding the scope of automation to creative, analytical, and interpersonal work domains. This signifies that AI's economic impact is extending beyond blue-collar occupations centered on manufacturing to white-collar occupations centered on services.

### 2.2 Economic Distinctiveness of Generative AI

The key economic characteristics that distinguish generative AI from previous technologies can be summarized in three points.

First, **generality (General Purpose)**. Traditional automation technologies were specialized for specific tasks, requiring separate technology investments by industry. In contrast, a single generative AI model can be applied to a wide range of fields including programming, legal document drafting, medical diagnosis assistance, and customer consultation. According to McKinsey's (2023) analysis, generative AI can create economic value in over 63 use cases, significantly broader in scope than previous analytical AI.

Second, **automation of cognitive tasks**. While past industrial robots replaced physical labor, generative AI complements or replaces cognitive labor such as analysis, judgment, and creation. Goldman Sachs (2023) estimates that approximately two-thirds of U.S. occupations are somewhat exposed to AI automation, and 25-50% of the workload of those occupations can be replaced by AI. This demands a fundamental reassessment of professional and managerial occupations traditionally considered safe from automation.

Third, **dramatic reduction in marginal costs**. Like digital technologies in general, generative AI services involve enormous initial development costs (GPT-4 training cost estimated at over $100M), but the inference costs of trained models continue to decline. This cost structure provides an economic foundation for AI utilization to rapidly expand from large corporations to SMEs and startups.

### 2.3 AI Investment Status and Trends

Global investment in AI is increasing exponentially. According to Stanford University's Human-Centered AI Institute (HAI) AI Index Report (2024), private investment in generative AI reached $25.2 billion in 2023, an approximately 8-fold increase from the previous year. Microsoft's New Future of Work Report (2025) reported that total global AI private investment reached $33.9 billion in 2024, an 18.7% increase year-over-year.

AI adoption is also accelerating at the corporate level. According to Amundi Research Center analysis, as of December 2024, more than 6.0% of U.S. companies are already utilizing AI in production processes, up significantly from 3.7% a year earlier. Enterprise generative AI usage surged from 55% in 2023 to 75% in 2024, and according to IDC (2024), companies achieve an average ROI of 3.7x for every dollar invested in generative AI, with leading companies recording 10.3x ROI.

According to Korea Eximbank's (2024) analysis, U.S. Big Tech companies are leading the era of hyperscale AI while widening the technology gap with other countries. This investment concentration phenomenon suggests that AI's economic effects may be unevenly distributed across countries and companies.

## Chapter 3. AI and Macroeconomics: GDP Growth Projections

### 3.1 Optimism: An Economic Golden Age Brought by AI

The most optimistic projections of AI's macroeconomic impact come primarily from global investment banks and consulting firms.

**Goldman Sachs'** research team projects that generative AI could increase global GDP by 7% (approximately $7 trillion) and boost productivity growth by 1.5 percentage points over the next decade. In the case of the United States, full-scale AI adoption could improve labor productivity by approximately 1.5 percentage points annually and accelerate potential GDP growth from an average of 2.1% in 2025-2029 to 2.3% in the early 2030s.

**McKinsey Global Institute** (2023) presents even more specific analysis. Generative AI could add $2.6-4.4 trillion in annual economic value across 63 use cases and contribute 0.1-0.6 percentage points annually to labor productivity growth. Combined with other automation technologies, it could contribute 0.5-3.4 percentage points to annual productivity growth. Furthermore, 60-70% of current worker hours can be automated, and half of today's work activities are estimated to become automated between 2030-2060 (median 2045).

**PwC** projects that AI could increase global economic output by up to 15 percentage points over the next decade, equivalent to an additional 1 percentage point in annual growth rate. According to PwC's Global AI Jobs Barometer (2025), workers with AI skills enjoy an average 56% wage premium, demonstrating that AI provides significant economic opportunities for highly skilled workers.

**IDC's** (2024) projection estimates that the cumulative global economic impact of AI-related business spending will reach $19.9 trillion by 2030, accounting for 3.5% of global GDP in that year.

**Penn Wharton Budget Model** analyzes AI's GDP impact from a long-term perspective, projecting GDP increases of 1.5% by 2035, approximately 3% by 2055, and 3.7% by 2075. This model estimates that approximately 40% of current GDP is potentially affected by generative AI, suggesting that AI's economic penetration could be extremely extensive.

### 3.2 Realism: Potential Overestimation of AI's Economic Effects

The most systematic critique of optimistic projections is presented by MIT Professor Daron Acemoglu (2024). Acemoglu argues that AI's impact on GDP will be "nontrivial but modest," estimating the GDP growth effect at approximately 1% over the next decade and a realistic maximum of approximately 1.1%.

Acemoglu presents the following grounds for this conservative projection. First, technology adoption requires considerable time. Historically, general-purpose technologies (GPTs) such as steam engines, electricity, and computers took decades to spread throughout the economy and translate into productivity improvements. AI will likely follow this pattern. Second, there is a gap between tasks that can be automated and tasks for which automation is economically desirable. Even if technically automatable, actual automation may be limited by factors such as implementation costs, organizational resistance, and regulation. Third, there are quality issues with AI-generated output. Problems such as generative AI 'hallucinations' make complete automation in fields requiring professional judgment difficult in the short term.

The Congressional Budget Office (CBO) also acknowledged that AI could affect economic growth, employment, and wages, while emphasizing that considerable uncertainty remains regarding the direction, magnitude, and timing of its impact.

### 3.3 Potential Recurrence of the Productivity Paradox

What is easily overlooked in current AI discussions is the potential recurrence of the 'Productivity Paradox.' In 1987, Robert Solow presented the famous paradox: "You can see the computer age everywhere but in the productivity statistics." For decades afterward, the phenomenon was observed where IT investment did not translate into macroscopic productivity improvements, and it was not until after the 2000s that IT's productivity enhancement effects manifested fully.

A similar paradox may be observed with AI. Despite massive investment, AI's effects may not clearly appear in macroscopic productivity indicators. This is because complementary investments accompanying AI adoption (organizational redesign, workforce retraining, infrastructure development) must proceed in parallel, and these transition costs may offset productivity improvements in the short term.

However, Amundi Research Center's analysis suggests that this paradox may be shorter-lived in the AI era. In the case of the United States, AI is projected to improve labor productivity by approximately 1 percentage point annually and contribute approximately 0.35 percentage points to GDP growth over the next decade, which could materialize more rapidly than digitalization's productivity effects.

### 3.4 Cross-Country GDP Impact Comparison

AI's economic impact is projected to vary significantly across countries.

**Advanced economies (U.S., EU, Japan, Korea, etc.)** are expected to enjoy AI's productivity enhancement effects earliest and most significantly, based on high technological infrastructure, abundant human capital, and developed AI ecosystems. The United States, in particular, is projected to be the biggest beneficiary of AI's economic effects due to advantages such as hosting major AI companies, the world's largest venture capital market, and excellent research talent.

**China** is emerging as the second AI powerhouse behind the United States, based on abundant data, the government's active AI industry promotion policies, and a vast domestic market. However, semiconductor export restrictions due to U.S.-China technology competition and data sovereignty issues may serve as variables.

For **developing countries**, according to IMF (2024) analysis, AI's growth effects may be limited to half the level of advanced economies, raising concerns about exacerbating inter-country income inequality. Key obstacles include insufficient digital infrastructure, AI talent drain, and lack of local language data. However, the World Bank reports that demand for AI skills in developing countries increased 9-fold between 2021 and 2024, suggesting the possibility of rapid catch-up by latecomers.

For **Korea**, AI adoption is progressing rapidly based on high IT infrastructure levels and educational enthusiasm, but gaps in AI adoption between large and small enterprises, the regulatory environment, and demographic structure remain challenging. Research on the effects of Korea's AI industry development on economic growth shows that AI industry development positively affects GDP growth with a lag of approximately one year, with government-oriented AI revenue having a greater impact on GDP growth than private sector revenue.

## Chapter 4. AI and the Labor Market: Automation and the Future of Work

### 4.1 New Character of AI Automation: White-Collar Focus

While past automation technologies primarily targeted simple repetitive tasks in manufacturing, automation by AI, especially generative AI, shows a fundamentally different pattern. The most prominent characteristic is the shift in automation targets from blue-collar to white-collar occupations.

According to Anthropic's 'AI's Impact on the Labor Market' report released in March 2026, the occupation most likely to be replaced by AI was computer programmers. Customer service representatives and data entry workers also showed high automation exposure. This contrasts with traditional automation targeting physical labor, suggesting that highly educated, high-income white-collar occupations may actually be at greater risk of AI displacement.

Penn Wharton Budget Model's analysis quantitatively confirms this trend. Occupations at approximately the 80th income percentile—upper-middle-class income levels—are most heavily exposed to generative AI automation. This is a pattern contrary to past automation, which primarily threatened low-income, low-skill occupations.

This trend is also observed in Korea. The Korea Institute for Industrial Economics and Trade warns that industrial transformation due to AI may have the power to replace labor demand very rapidly and comprehensively, unlike previous automation. While previous mechanization and automation progressed gradually over decades, the automation of cognitive labor by AI presents a qualitatively different challenge in that it can progress at the speed of software deployment.

### 4.2 Occupation-Specific Displacement Risk Analysis

Occupational exposure to AI automation varies greatly depending on the nature of work. According to Goldman Sachs' (2023) analysis, approximately two-thirds of U.S. occupations are somewhat exposed to AI automation, with 25% to 50% of the workload of those occupations replaceable by AI.

**High-risk occupations** include administrative support, legal assistance, financial analysis, customer service, data entry, and basic programming. These occupations have core duties consisting of information processing according to standardized rules, pattern recognition, text generation, and other tasks that AI performs well.

**Medium-risk occupations** include medical professionals, educators, engineers, and managers. In these occupations, AI can complement or streamline portions of work, but core tasks requiring human judgment, empathy, and creativity are difficult to replace in the short term.

**Low-risk occupations** are those with extensive physical on-site work such as construction, maintenance, caregiving, and cooking. These occupations consist of non-routine physical tasks that are difficult to automate at current AI and robotics technology levels.

The IMF (2024) estimates that globally, approximately 40% of employment is exposed to AI, with this proportion being higher in advanced economies. In advanced economies, approximately 30% of jobs face high-risk displacement by AI, compared to 20% in emerging markets and 18% in low-income countries. This is because advanced economies' economic structures are concentrated in services and knowledge economies, making them more susceptible to AI's cognitive automation impact.

### 4.3 New Job Creation vs. Existing Job Displacement

AI's labor market impact cannot be reduced to simple job displacement and must be understood within the dynamic relationship with new job creation.

According to the World Economic Forum (WEF) Future of Jobs Report 2025, structural labor market transformation between 2025 and 2030 is projected to affect 22% of current jobs. Specifically, 170 million new jobs will be created and 92 million jobs displaced, yielding a net increase of 78 million jobs (7%).

Despite this net-positive projection, 'transition costs' may be substantial. When workers from displaced jobs lack the competencies suited for newly created positions, structural unemployment results. The competencies required in the AI era—analytical, technical, and creative abilities—are qualitatively different from existing routine task performance capabilities.

According to Harvard Business School research, after ChatGPT's release, job postings for occupations with high automation potential decreased by 13%, while hiring demand for positions requiring analytical, technical, and creative work increased by 20%. This demonstrates that AI is not simply replacing jobs but reshaping the demand structure of the labor market itself.

MIT's research team emphasizes that AI's impact operates at the level of specific tasks within occupations, not at the level of entire occupations. That is, in most cases, AI does not replace entire occupations but automates AI-suitable tasks within occupations, enabling workers to concentrate on tasks unsuitable for AI. This 'task reallocation' perspective contributes to a more precise understanding of AI's labor market impact.

### 4.4 Skills Gap and Wage Premium

One of the key labor market phenomena of the AI era is the widening wage gap between workers who possess AI-related skills and those who do not.

According to PwC's Global AI Jobs Barometer (2025), workers with AI skills enjoy an average 56% wage premium. This premium is observed not only in AI-specific occupations but across various occupations that enhance work productivity through AI tools. Interestingly, wages have actually risen in occupations with high automation potential, such as customer service, suggesting that AI is serving an augmentation role rather than simple replacement in these occupations.

New skills are emerging in digital, cognitive, and creative domains, emphasizing the importance of flexible adaptability and lifelong learning. Current educational systems are insufficient for cultivating the competencies required in the AI era, making the expansion of reskilling and upskilling programs an urgent priority.

### 4.5 Implications for the Korean Labor Market

Korea's labor market simultaneously possesses particular vulnerabilities and opportunities in the AI era.

According to the Korea Labor Institute report, youth employment indicators in occupations with high AI exposure decreased by 15% between 2022 and 2024. Additionally, companies with higher AI adoption rates have seen stagnating youth employment growth rates since 2023. This suggests that AI adoption is affecting the labor market through reduced new hiring, with particularly greater impacts on young people entering the labor market.

According to KDI analysis, as of 2023, it is estimated that technically 70% or more of tasks can be automated in 38.8% of domestic jobs. This figure reflects Korea's high service industry share and digital infrastructure level, significantly exceeding the OECD average of approximately 27%.

However, Anthropic's research points out that AI's impact is not yet statistically clear in unemployment data, finding anomalies only in youth employment data. This suggests that AI's labor market impact is still in its early stages, and its full effects may gradually emerge over the next 3-5 years.

In Korea's case, rapid aging and declining working-age population trends may serve as positive factors for AI adoption. In an environment of worsening labor shortages, AI could become a key means of maintaining productivity, meaning AI could play a role of 'filling' rather than 'taking' jobs.

## Chapter 5. AI and Industrial Structure Transformation

### 5.1 Differential Industry-Level Impacts of AI Adoption

AI's impact on industrial structure varies markedly by industry. According to McKinsey's (2023) analysis, the industries with the greatest potential for AI economic value creation are, in order: financial services, technology/media/telecommunications, life sciences/pharmaceuticals, and professional services (consulting, legal, accounting).

**Financial services** is at the forefront of AI adoption. AI utilization is rapidly expanding in risk assessment, fraud detection, algorithmic trading, and personalized financial product recommendations. With the advent of generative AI, automation of cognitive tasks such as report writing, customer communications, and regulatory compliance document generation is also accelerating.

In the **healthcare industry**, AI is being utilized for improving diagnostic accuracy, shortening drug development timelines, and designing personalized treatments. AI-based medical image analysis has achieved specialist-level diagnostic accuracy in some areas, and AI-assisted drug candidate discovery can shorten development timelines by several years compared to traditional methods.

In **manufacturing**, AI is utilized for predictive maintenance, quality control, and supply chain optimization, innovatively improving manufacturing process efficiency in combination with Digital Twin technology. However, AI adoption in manufacturing sites centered on physical work depends on the pace of robotics technology advancement.

**Agriculture** is one of the industries where AI adoption has been relatively slow, but with advances in Precision Agriculture technology, AI utilization is gradually expanding in early crop disease detection, optimal irrigation and fertilization planning, and yield prediction.

### 5.2 Rise of AI-Native Industries

Entirely new industrial domains are forming based on AI technology. So-called 'AI-Native' industries refer to industries where AI constitutes the core of the business model rather than being used as an auxiliary tool.

Representative AI-native industries include AI model development and services (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, etc.), AI infrastructure (GPU chips, cloud computing, data centers), AI-based content creation (images, video, music, text), AI security and safety (AI monitoring, bias detection, safety evaluation), and autonomous systems (autonomous driving, drones, robots).

According to Stanford HAI (2024), private investment in generative AI reaching $25.2 billion in 2023—an 8-fold increase from the previous year—reflects the explosive growth of this field. Microsoft's (2025) report indicates that global AI private investment reached $33.9 billion in 2024, showing that AI-native industries' share of the global economy is rapidly expanding.

### 5.3 AI Transformation Strategy for Traditional Industries

AI adoption in traditional industries requires fundamental business model transformation beyond simple technology adoption. According to IDC (2024), companies that have invested intensively in AI achieve an average ROI of 3.7x per dollar invested in generative AI, with the leading company group recording 10.3x ROI. This ROI gap suggests that AI adoption success is determined more by organizational strategy, culture, and capabilities than by the technology itself.

Key elements for successful AI transformation include: first, data infrastructure development is necessary, as AI performance heavily depends on the quality and quantity of training data; second, human capital restructuring is required, including improving existing workforce AI literacy and securing AI specialists; and third, organizational culture change must be accompanied, as AI adoption demands changes in existing decision-making processes, workflows, and performance evaluation systems.

### 5.4 Combination of Platform Economy and AI

AI is reinforcing and expanding the existing digital platform economy. Developing large-scale AI models requires massive computing resources and data, which can further consolidate the competitive advantage of Big Tech companies that already possess such resources.

According to Korea Eximbank's (2024) analysis, U.S. Big Tech companies are leading the hyperscale AI era while widening the gap with companies from other countries. Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Meta, and others are establishing dominant positions in the AI ecosystem by vertically integrating the entire value chain of AI model development, cloud infrastructure, and application services.

This phenomenon may strengthen 'Winner Takes All' tendencies and could weaken the competitiveness of SMEs and startups. However, simultaneously, the proliferation of open-source AI models (Meta's Llama, Google's Gemma, etc.) and the universalization of API-based AI services also have the effect of lowering entry barriers, showing a pattern where both monopolization and democratization of the platform economy proceed simultaneously.

## Chapter 6. AI and Income Inequality

### 6.1 Skill-Biased Technological Change and Income Polarization

Income inequality in the AI era can be understood as an extension of the 'Skill-Biased Technological Change (SBTC)' theory. According to SBTC theory, new technologies increase demand for high-skilled labor and decrease demand for low-skilled labor, thereby widening the income gap.

However, technological change driven by AI shows a different pattern from traditional SBTC. As discussed earlier, generative AI actually poses a greater threat to high-skilled, high-income occupations. According to Penn Wharton's analysis, occupations at the 80th income percentile are most heavily exposed to AI automation. This means that AI is having differential impacts based on the distinction between 'routine cognitive tasks and non-routine tasks' rather than the traditional 'skilled-unskilled' distinction.

Consequently, income polarization in the AI era may have a dual structure. On one hand, high-skilled workers and AI specialists who can effectively utilize AI will enjoy high wage premiums. On the other hand, middle- and high-income workers whose tasks are automated by AI but who find it difficult to transition to AI-leveraged work may emerge as a new vulnerable class.

### 6.2 AI Skill Premium and Labor Market Dualization

According to PwC's (2025) analysis, workers with AI skills enjoy an average 56% wage premium. This premium is not limited to AI developers or data scientists but is observed across various occupations that enhance work productivity using AI tools.

The existence of this AI skill premium may promote the dualization of the labor market. As the productivity gap between 'digitally skilled' workers equipped with AI utilization capabilities and 'digitally unskilled' workers widens, this inevitably leads to differences in wage gaps and employment stability.

A particular concern is that this AI skill gap may overlap with existing socioeconomic inequalities. Groups with socioeconomic conditions providing access to quality digital education are more likely to benefit from the AI era, while those without such conditions may experience deepened economic marginalization. This suggests that income inequality driven by AI could form a vicious cycle reinforcing existing educational, income, and regional disparities.

### 6.3 International Digital Divide and AI Divide

The potential for uneven distribution of AI's economic effects across countries is a factor raising concerns about global-level inequality.

According to IMF (2024) analysis, AI's economic growth effects are greatest in advanced economies, while in low-income countries, the effects may be limited to half the level of advanced economies. This means that existing gaps in digital infrastructure, human capital, and institutional environments may be further widened in the AI era.

Specifically, approximately 60% of jobs in advanced economies are exposed to AI, but half of those could see their work augmented by AI, potentially improving productivity and wages. In contrast, in low-income countries, AI exposure is low at 26%, so the direct risk of job losses from automation is small, but simultaneously the benefits of AI-driven productivity improvements are also limited.

The World Bank reports that demand for AI skills in developing countries increased 9-fold between 2021 and 2024. In upper-middle-income countries, AI skill demand increased by 16%, and in lower-middle-income countries by 11%, while in high-income countries it increased by only 2%. This suggests the possibility of rapid catch-up by late starters, but absolute gaps in AI capability levels remain substantial.

### 6.4 Concentration of Wealth in the AI Era

The development of AI technology can fundamentally change the structure of economic value production and distribution. The enormous capital required for AI model development (computing infrastructure, data, talent) is concentrated in a handful of large corporations, and a significant portion of the economic value created by AI may accrue to these companies and their shareholders.

The market capitalizations of Big Tech companies such as Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Meta, and Apple have increased to unprecedented levels alongside the AI boom, serving as a factor widening the wealth gap between capital owners and labor providers. If the fruits of AI-driven productivity improvements are distributed more in the form of corporate profits and stock price increases rather than wage increases, changes in the 'capital-labor' distribution ratio could cause structural deepening of income inequality.

This phenomenon of wealth concentration may also serve as a threat to democracy and social stability. As the ownership and control of AI technology becomes concentrated in the hands of a few, the democratic nature of economic decision-making may weaken and social conflict may intensify.

## Chapter 7. Economic Policy Challenges in the AI Era

### 7.1 Education and Human Capital Policy

The most urgent policy challenge of the AI era is the fundamental redesign of the education system. The current education system was designed for the demands of the industrial era, focused on transmitting standardized knowledge and cultivating routine task performance abilities. However, in an era when AI automates routine cognitive tasks, competencies that AI finds difficult to perform—critical thinking, creativity, complex problem-solving, and social/emotional intelligence—become core competitiveness.

WEF's Future of Jobs Report 2025 projects that demand for analytical, technical, and creative competencies will surge around 2025-2030 and emphasizes the need for large-scale reskilling of existing workers. Specific education policy directions include:

First, universalization of AI literacy. Basic understanding of AI's principles, possibilities, and limitations is becoming a necessary competency for all citizens. AI literacy should be integrated into primary, secondary, and higher education curricula, and AI application training for non-specialists should be expanded.

Second, establishing lifelong learning systems. Given the rapid pace of AI technology development, education at a single point in time is insufficient. Institutional support enabling continuous learning throughout one's career (learning leave, education vouchers, online learning platforms, etc.) is needed.

Third, innovation in university education. The rigidity of disciplinary divisions should be relaxed, and convergence education combining AI with humanities, social sciences, and arts should be strengthened. Innovation in educational methodology utilizing AI should also be pursued.

### 7.2 Social Safety Net Redesign

Job transitions caused by AI demand a fundamental reexamination of existing social safety net systems. Traditional social insurance systems were designed premised on economic structures approaching 'full employment' and long-term employment, which may not be suitable for the fluid labor markets of the AI era.

Both the IMF and World Bank emphasize the need for strategic policy intervention to strengthen social safety nets in the AI era. Specific policy directions include the expansion of unemployment insurance and strengthening of career transition support services. Workers who lose jobs due to AI need income protection and vocational training support during the process of transitioning to new occupations.

The discussion of Universal Basic Income (UBI) also takes on a new context in the AI era. If large-scale job displacement by AI materializes, traditional employment-centered income distribution systems alone may be insufficient to guarantee the basic livelihood of society members. Full-scale policy experimentation and research into alternative income guarantee systems such as UBI or Negative Income Tax are needed.

New forms of social security systems responding to the spread of the gig economy and platform labor must also be established. Employment-neutral social safety nets should be designed so that workers receive adequate social protection even in freelance and project-based employment forms promoted by AI.

### 7.3 AI Governance and Regulation

As AI's economic utilization expands, establishing appropriate governance and regulatory frameworks has emerged as an important policy challenge. The core regulatory challenge is balancing 'promoting innovation' with 'managing risk.'

The EU's AI Act adopts a risk-based approach, classifying AI systems by risk level and applying differential regulation. The U.S. presents guidelines on AI safety and security through executive orders while placing relatively greater emphasis on private sector self-regulation. China has more direct regulatory intervention through measures such as interim regulations on generative AI service management.

In Korea's case, a flexible approach utilizing 'regulatory sandboxes' is needed for AI regulation. Excessive regulation can hinder AI industry development, while insufficient regulation can increase social risks. Specific guidelines for AI utilization in key areas such as employment/labor, finance, and healthcare are urgently needed.

### 7.4 Tax Policy in the AI Era

Economic structural changes driven by AI also have important implications for the tax system. If AI replaces labor and increases the share of capital income, the current tax system centered on labor income could face erosion of its tax base.

Policy responses being discussed include a Robot Tax or automation tax—imposing taxes on labor displaced by AI or robots, as proposed by Bill Gates and others. However, criticism exists that this could suppress AI adoption and hinder productivity improvements.

A Digital Tax seeks to achieve fair taxation on value creation in the AI era by imposing appropriate taxes on Big Tech companies' digital service revenues. International agreement is progressing under the OECD's BEPS framework.

Strengthening capital gains taxes to counter the concentration of wealth in capital income during the AI era is also being discussed, seeking to redistribute the fruits of AI-driven productivity improvements through the tax system when they are concentrated among shareholders and capital owners.

### 7.5 International Cooperation Framework

As AI's economic impact transcends borders, establishing international cooperation systems is essential. The World Bank emphasizes that for developing countries to equitably enjoy AI's benefits, four foundational elements must be strengthened: connectivity, compute, context, and competency.

Specific areas for international cooperation include international standardization of AI safety standards, technology transfer and capacity-building support for developing countries, establishing international governance systems to prevent AI-driven inter-country inequality, and international coordination of AI-related trade norms and data governance.

## Chapter 8. Reorganization of the Global Economic Order

### 8.1 U.S.-China AI Hegemony Competition and Economic Implications

AI is one of the most powerful drivers reshaping the 21st-century global economic order, and at its center lies the U.S.-China AI hegemony competition.

The United States holds overwhelming superiority as the home of major AI companies (OpenAI, Google, Anthropic, Meta, etc.) in AI model development, semiconductor design (NVIDIA, AMD), and cloud infrastructure. According to Korea Eximbank's (2024) analysis, U.S. Big Tech companies are leading the hyperscale AI era while widening the technology gap with other countries.

China is pursuing the United States in AI based on abundant data, strong government industrial policy, and a large domestic market. However, U.S. semiconductor export restrictions are imposing significant constraints on China's AI development, and the resulting technological decoupling could bifurcate the global AI ecosystem.

The economic implications of the U.S.-China AI competition are multilayered. In the short term, both countries are accelerating AI investment, promoting technological innovation and economic growth. However, in the long term, fragmentation of technology ecosystems could reduce the efficiency of global trade and investment, and there are concerns that a 'technology cold war' could strengthen economic bloc formation.

### 8.2 AI and Changes in Trade Structure

AI can bring profound changes to global trade structure. First, automation by AI may promote reshoring in advanced economies. Manufacturing activities that were previously relocated to developing countries to utilize cheaper labor may return home due to automation by AI and robots. This could threaten developing countries' manufacturing-based growth models.

Second, the share of digital services trade is expected to surge. AI-based services can be provided across borders without physical movement, potentially fundamentally changing the nature and scale of services trade.

Third, data is emerging as a new trade resource. In the AI era, data is a key resource comparable to oil, and differences in cross-country norms regarding data collection, processing, and utilization are serving as new sources of trade friction.

### 8.3 Opportunities and Crises for Developing Countries

In the AI era, developing countries simultaneously face both opportunities and crises.

Risk factors include manufacturing job outflows due to AI-driven reshoring, limited AI accessibility due to insufficient digital infrastructure, AI talent drain to advanced economies, and deepening inter-country income inequality due to widening productivity gaps from AI.

Opportunity factors include, as emphasized by the World Bank, the explosive growth in AI skill demand in developing countries (9-fold increase in 2021-2024), suggesting the possibility of leapfrogging development through AI utilization. Additionally, AI-based agriculture, healthcare, and education solutions can contribute to solving key challenges facing developing countries. The spread of open-source AI models is a positive factor for improving AI accessibility in developing countries.

The IMF emphasizes that to realize these opportunities, investment in digital infrastructure, innovation of education systems, and establishment of data governance systems are urgently needed in developing countries, and proposes that international community support and cooperation are essential.

## Chapter 9. Implications for the Korean Economy

### 9.1 Current State of Korea's AI Industry

Korea possesses considerable potential in the AI era based on its high-level IT infrastructure, educational enthusiasm, and manufacturing competitiveness. World-class internet speeds, high smartphone penetration, and strengths in the semiconductor industry (Samsung Electronics, SK hynix) are important competitive assets in the AI era.

However, competitiveness in core areas of the AI ecosystem is limited compared to the U.S. and China. As Korea Eximbank's (2024) analysis points out, the gap with U.S. Big Tech in hyperscale AI model development is widening, and Korea significantly lags behind the U.S. and China in the depth of its AI startup ecosystem and the scale of venture investment.

Research on the effects of Korea's AI industry development on economic growth shows that AI industry development positively affects GDP growth with a lag of approximately one year, with government-oriented AI revenue having a greater impact on GDP growth than private enterprise or consumer-facing AI revenue. This suggests that the government's AI industry promotion policies are translating into economic outcomes.

### 9.2 Structural Vulnerabilities of Korea's Labor Market

Korea's labor market has several structural vulnerabilities in the AI era.

First, according to KDI analysis, it is estimated that 70% or more of tasks can be automated in 38.8% of domestic jobs, significantly exceeding the OECD average. The paradoxical situation is that a high service sector share and developed digital infrastructure actually increase exposure to AI automation.

Second, as confirmed by the Korea Labor Institute report, the fact that youth employment in occupations with high AI exposure is already declining shows that AI's employment impact is materializing. A 15% decrease in relevant occupational youth employment indicators between 2022-2024 is a figure that cannot be ignored.

Third, Korea's high university enrollment rate and credential inflation may create new problems in the AI era. If highly educated white-collar occupations are most heavily threatened by AI, enormous investment in higher education may fail to provide expected economic returns.

Fourth, the AI adoption gap between large corporations and SMEs may reinforce labor market dualization. While large corporations improve productivity and profitability through AI adoption, SMEs lacking AI investment capacity may face weakened competitiveness, potentially widening the wage gap between large and small enterprises.

### 9.3 Korea's AI Economic Strategy: Seizing Opportunities

Strategic directions for Korea to seize economic opportunities in the AI era can be presented as follows.

**First, advancing AI-utilizing industries.** In manufacturing sectors where Korea has strengths—semiconductors, displays, electronics, automobiles, shipbuilding—productivity innovation and high-value-addition through AI utilization should be pursued. An 'Industrial AI' strategy that leverages AI as a core base technology to elevate existing industrial competitiveness could be effective.

**Second, expanding AI talent development.** The education system should be reformed so that not only AI majors but personnel in all fields can acquire AI utilization capabilities. This requires expanding AI convergence majors in university education, strengthening AI application training programs for current workers, and institutional support for attracting overseas AI talent.

**Third, AI strategy responsive to demographic change.** Korea's rapid aging and declining working-age population increase the imperative for AI adoption. A strategy of supplementing labor shortages due to population decline through AI enables a shift in perception from concerns about AI taking jobs to the recognition that AI is essential for maintaining economic sustainability.

**Fourth, supporting SME AI adoption.** To bridge the AI gap between large corporations and SMEs, policies providing financial support, technical consulting, and shared AI platforms for SME AI adoption are needed. This should ensure that AI's economic effects spread throughout the economy.

**Fifth, advancing the AI regulatory environment.** A balanced AI regulatory system that promotes innovation while ensuring social safety should be established. Active utilization of regulatory sandboxes, establishment of sector-specific AI guidelines, and preemptive response to international regulatory trends are required.

## Chapter 10. Conclusions and Recommendations

### 10.1 Summary of Key Findings

This study conducted a multidimensional analysis of the economic future of the AI era from the perspectives of macroeconomics, labor markets, industrial structure, income inequality, policy, and global order.

First, regarding AI's macroeconomic impact, considerable divergence exists between optimistic (Goldman Sachs' 7% global GDP increase) and realistic (Acemoglu's 1% increase over 10 years) projections, and the actual impact will be determined by the speed of technology adoption, complementary investments, and institutional responses. However, consensus has formed that AI will have a substantial impact on the economy.

Second, AI's labor market impact is qualitatively different from past automation. White-collar, high-income occupations are actually more heavily threatened, and while the WEF projects a net increase of 78 million jobs, transition costs may be substantial. The 56% wage premium for workers with AI skills warns of deepening labor market polarization.

Third, in industrial structure, the rise of AI-native industries and AI transformation of traditional industries are proceeding simultaneously, with platform companies' dominance strengthening and democratization through open-source running in parallel.

Fourth, income inequality may deepen through multilayered mechanisms including AI skill gaps, international digital divides, and changes in capital-labor distribution ratios.

Fifth, the Korean economy faces the dual situation of high AI automation exposure (38.8%) combined with the positive necessity of AI adoption due to aging demographics.

### 10.2 Comprehensive Assessment: AI Economy Scenarios

Synthesizing the analysis of this study, the economic future of the AI era can be broadly classified into three scenarios.

**Scenario 1: Inclusive Growth.** When AI's productivity enhancement effects spread broadly and transition costs are minimized through appropriate education policies and social safety nets. In this scenario, AI-driven GDP increases approach McKinsey's projections (annual $2.6T-$4.4T), new jobs offset displaced ones, and income inequality is maintained at manageable levels.

**Scenario 2: Polarized Prosperity.** When AI's economic effects materialize but their benefits concentrate among the few who own and can utilize AI technology. Macroeconomic indicators show growth but are accompanied by side effects including deepening income inequality, increasing social conflict, and political instability.

**Scenario 3: Stagnant Transition.** When AI's economic effects are more limited than expected, as in Acemoglu's projections, and transition costs offset productivity improvements with the 'productivity paradox' recurring. Despite massive AI investment, improvements in macroscopic productivity indicators are minimal and labor market disruption only intensifies.

Which scenario materializes is more likely to be determined by the speed and direction of institutional responses than by the technology itself. This means that AI's economic future is not technologically determined but can be socially constructed.

### 10.3 Policy Recommendations

Based on the analysis of this study, the following policy recommendations are presented.

1. **Fundamental Redesign of the Education System:** AI literacy should be integrated into basic education and lifelong learning systems established so that all citizens can acquire the competencies to adapt to the AI era.

2. **Inclusive AI Transition Policies:** To prevent AI's economic benefits from concentrating among the few, SME AI adoption support, retraining programs, and career transition services should be expanded.

3. **Future-Oriented Social Safety Net:** New forms of social security systems suitable for the fluid labor markets of the AI era should be designed.

4. **Balanced AI Regulation:** A smart regulatory system that promotes innovation while managing social risks should be established.

5. **Active International Cooperation:** To prevent the deepening of inter-country inequality due to AI, active participation in technology transfer, capacity building, and the establishment of international governance systems is necessary.

6. **Korea-Specific AI Economic Strategy:** An industrial AI strategy leveraging Korea's strengths (semiconductors, manufacturing, IT infrastructure) should be pursued, and an AI utilization strategy responsive to aging and population decline should be developed.

### 10.4 Research Limitations and Future Tasks

This study is based on a systematic literature review but has the following limitations. First, due to the extremely rapid pace of AI technology development, technological changes after the time of this analysis may significantly revise projections. Second, due to the nature of literature-based research, independent quantitative analysis or empirical research was not conducted. Third, analysis of AGI emergence and its economic impact is limited.

Future research requires independent quantitative analysis of AI's impact on the Korean economy, granular impact analysis by industry and occupation, and economic analysis of AGI scenarios.
