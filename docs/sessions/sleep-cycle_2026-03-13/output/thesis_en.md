# Mechanisms of Sleep Cycles and Their Health Impact: Recent Research Trends

## Abstract

This study systematically reviews the physiological mechanisms of sleep cycles and their impact on health. Sleep is a repetitive process of approximately 90-minute cycles composed of NREM and REM stages, governed by a dual regulatory model of circadian rhythm and homeostatic sleep pressure. Previous research indicates that disruption of sleep cycles is associated with various health problems including cognitive decline, metabolic disorders, and cardiovascular disease (Hill et al., 2018), and from a chronotherapy perspective, restoration of circadian rhythm is emerging as a novel therapeutic strategy (Lee et al., 2021). This paper discusses the basic structure, regulatory mechanisms, health impacts, and improvement strategies of sleep cycles.

## 1. Introduction

Sleep is an essential physiological process for human survival, occupying approximately one-third of the day. During sleep, the brain organizes information while the body performs recovery and immune function enhancement. However, in modern society, sleep deprivation and deterioration of sleep quality have emerged as serious public health issues.

According to Hill et al. (2018), stress and fatigue increase sleep needs, and insufficient sleep in turn exacerbates stress responses, creating a vicious cycle. Lerman et al. (2012) reported in their workplace fatigue risk management study that regularity of sleep cycles is directly linked to workplace safety.

This study aims to answer the following questions: (1) What stages compose the sleep cycle? (2) What are the physiological mechanisms regulating sleep cycles? (3) What are the health impacts of sleep cycle disruption?

## 2. Structure of Sleep Cycles

### 2.1 NREM Sleep

NREM (Non-Rapid Eye Movement) sleep is divided into three stages. Stage N1 is the transition from wakefulness to sleep, during which brain waves change from alpha to theta waves. In Stage N2, sleep spindles and K-complexes appear, and this stage accounts for approximately 45-55% of total sleep. Stage N3 is slow-wave sleep, characterized by dominant delta waves, during which physical recovery and growth hormone secretion are concentrated.

According to Alam et al.'s (2022) electrophysiological study, the transition of the sleep-wake cycle is regulated by a flip-flop switch mechanism of mutual inhibition between the ventrolateral preoptic nucleus (VLPO) of the hypothalamus and wake-promoting neurons.

### 2.2 REM Sleep

REM (Rapid Eye Movement) sleep accounts for approximately 20-25% of total sleep and is the stage during which vivid dreams occur. In this stage, brain activity is similar to the waking state, but skeletal muscles are in a state of temporary paralysis (atonia). REM sleep has been reported to play important roles in memory consolidation, emotional processing, and creative problem-solving.

### 2.3 Sleep Cycles

A single sleep cycle progresses in the order of NREM → REM for approximately 90 minutes, and healthy adults repeat this 4-6 times per night. In the first half of the night, the proportion of N3 slow-wave sleep is higher, while the proportion of REM sleep increases toward the latter half.

## 3. Regulatory Mechanisms

Sleep cycles are explained by Borbély's Two-Process Model. **Process S** is homeostatic sleep pressure, where sleep drive increases through adenosine accumulation as waking time extends. **Process C** is the circadian rhythm, where the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) generates approximately 24-hour cycles based on light information.

Lee et al. (2021) analyzed the relationship between circadian rhythm and disease, explaining that SCN clock genes (CLOCK, BMAL1, PER, CRY) form approximately 24-hour transcription-translation feedback loops at the cellular level. Disruption of this system has been reported to increase the risk of metabolic disorders, cancer, and psychiatric disorders.

## 4. Health Impact of Sleep Cycle Disruption

Disruption of sleep cycles causes various health problems. According to Hill et al. (2018), chronic sleep deprivation is associated with cognitive decline, emotional dysregulation, and immune dysfunction. Lerman et al.'s (2012) workplace fatigue management study reported that sleep cycle disruption in shift workers increases accident risk by 2-3 times.

Wiggs & France (2000) reported that sleep problems frequently occur in children and adolescents with physical disabilities, and that behavioral interventions (sleep hygiene education, gradual extinction methods, etc.) are effective in stabilizing sleep cycles. This suggests that sleep cycle improvement is possible through behavioral approaches in addition to pharmacological approaches.

## 5. Conclusion

Sleep cycles consist of approximately 90-minute NREM-REM repetitions and are dually regulated by homeostatic sleep pressure and circadian rhythm. Disruption of sleep cycles has widespread effects on cognition, metabolism, cardiovascular health, and mental health, and chronotherapeutic approaches are receiving attention as new treatment strategies (Lee et al., 2021).

Future research should develop personalized sleep improvement strategies tailored to individual circadian rhythm characteristics (chronotypes). Additionally, research on real-time sleep cycle monitoring using wearable devices and intelligent sleep environment optimization based on such monitoring should be pursued.
